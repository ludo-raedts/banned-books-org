# Banned, Censored & Restricted Books: International Reference for banned-books.org

**TL;DR**
- The single most comprehensive starting point outside the U.S. is Wikipedia's master list "List of books banned by governments" (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_books_banned_by_governments), supplemented by country-specific Wikipedia "List of books banned in …" pages (India, Nazi Germany, Index Librorum Prohibitorum, etc.) — these alone yield several hundred concrete titles with dates and reasons and should anchor the database.
- The richest non-Wikipedia primary sources are Index on Censorship (indexoncensorship.org), PEN International country reports, Freemuse, Article 19, Human Rights Watch, IFEX, and Amnesty International's annual Banned Books Week booklist (amnestyusa.org/toolkits/banned-books-week-booklist); for regional depth use AUC Press (Arab world), ARABLIT (arablit.org), the Center for Human Rights in Iran (iranhumanrights.org), SAHistory Online, and the Beacon for Freedom of Expression database (search.beaconforfreedom.org).
- High-volume historical bans cluster in five buckets you should treat as core collections: (1) the Catholic Index Librorum Prohibitorum 1559–1966 (~4,000+ titles), (2) Nazi Germany 1933 (Wolfgang Herrmann's "Schwarze Liste"), (3) Apartheid South Africa 1950–1990 (~26,000 titles under the Publications Act), (4) Soviet Union/Glavlit and Eastern Bloc 1922–1991 (samizdat universe), and (5) Republic of Türkiye since 1923 (Emin Karaca documents ~20,000 banned works).

---

## Key Findings

1. **Wikipedia is the single best starting node.** The article "List of books banned by governments" includes country-by-country tables for 60+ jurisdictions with specific titles, years and reasons; the article "List of authors banned in Nazi Germany" links to hundreds of authors; "List of authors and works on the Index Librorum Prohibitorum" lists every condemned work 1559–1966. Each country block below points to the relevant page.

2. **The Satanic Verses (Salman Rushdie, 1988)** is the most globally banned modern book. It is or has been banned in: India (first country to ban it, October 1988), Iran (1989 fatwa by Khomeini), Pakistan, Bangladesh, Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Sudan, Somalia, Qatar, Kuwait, Malaysia, Indonesia, Sri Lanka, Singapore, Thailand, Tanzania, Kenya, Liberia, Sierra Leone, Papua New Guinea, Venezuela, and historically South Africa. Translator Hitoshi Igarashi was murdered in Japan (1991); Italian translator Ettore Capriolo was stabbed (1991); Norwegian publisher William Nygaard was shot (1993).

3. **Three regimes produced industrial-scale book destruction**: Nazi Germany's 10 May 1933 book burnings across 22 university towns (Wolfgang Herrmann's blacklist), Apartheid South Africa's Publications Act 1974 (26,000+ titles 1950–1990 per UCT Libraries), and Pinochet's Chile (mass military pyres of "Marxist cancer" 1973 onward — see Wikipedia "Book burnings in Chile").

4. **Distinct legal architectures** matter for the database schema: (a) state forfeiture statutes (India § 95 CrPC; Pakistan PCTB Act 2015; Turkey Anti-Terror Law); (b) religious authority bans (Vatican Index, Al-Azhar fatwas, Iran's Ministry of Culture and Islamic Guidance); (c) party-state pre-publication censorship (USSR's Glavlit, GDR's HV Verlage, China's GAPP); (d) customs/import bans (Australia 1901–1973; Canada – Little Sister's; Israel's enemy-states import ban); (e) school-district removals (US-style, also Malaysia 2020 list of LGBT titles).

5. **Multiple authors have suffered prosecution, imprisonment, exile or murder for books**: Naguib Mahfouz (stabbed 1994 over *Children of the Alley*); Theo van Gogh (murdered 2004); Hitoshi Igarashi (1991); Aziz Nesin (Sivas hotel arson attempt 1993); Ashraf Fayadh (Saudi death sentence commuted to 8 years + 800 lashes); Liu Xiaobo (China, Nobel laureate, died in custody 2017); Ahmed Naji (Egypt, 2-year sentence 2016); Aslı Erdoğan (Turkey, jailed 2016); Perumal Murugan (India, "death" of the author 2015).

## Recommendations (Concrete next steps for banned-books.org)

1. **Seed the database from Wikipedia first.** Programmatically scrape four cornerstone Wikipedia pages and parse their tables (most are already structured by columns Title / Author / Year / Notes):
   - https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_books_banned_by_governments (master)
   - https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_books_banned_in_India
   - https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_authors_and_works_on_the_Index_Librorum_Prohibitorum
   - https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_authors_banned_in_Nazi_Germany
   This yields ~1,500+ entries on day one. Use the Wikidata item Q17051374 ("list of books banned in India") and Q118947594 ("books banned in Turkey") to traverse linked-data cross-country relations.

2. **Layer in NGO datasets second.** Pull Amnesty International's annual "Banned Books Week Booklist" spreadsheet (https://www.amnestyusa.org/toolkits/banned-books-week-booklist/) which lists imprisoned/killed authors with their works; pull PEN International's "Day of the Imprisoned Writer" cases; pull Index on Censorship's tag archive; mirror Freemuse case lists.

3. **Country deep-dives via specialised primary sources.** For each priority country use these "first-call" archives:
   - India: SFLC.in (https://sflc.in/read-me-not-list-banned-books-india/) + Chennai Customs Parts 1–3.
   - Pakistan: Geo News / Dawn / Aqeel Abbas Jafri *Ma'atoob Kitaben* (Varsa Publications, Karachi).
   - Iran: Center for Human Rights in Iran + IranWire censorship tag.
   - Egypt/Arab world: AUC Press blog + ARABLIT.
   - Chile/Argentina/Brazil: Memoria Chilena, CONADEP archive, Brasil Nunca Mais report.
   - South Africa: UCT Libraries "Banned Books" collection + SAHistory.
   - Turkey: Emin Karaca, *Vaaay Kitabın Başına Gelenler!* + indices in Turkish; PEN Turkey.
   - Nazi Germany: NSDoku München blacklist database; USHMM "Library of Burned Books."
   - USSR/Eastern Bloc: OSA Archive Budapest + RFE/RL research collection.

4. **Schema priorities.** Capture for every entry: (a) original-language title + transliteration + English; (b) author + birth-death dates + nationality; (c) jurisdiction (country & sub-jurisdiction such as state/province); (d) issuing authority (court, ministry, customs, religious body, school board); (e) statute or order cited; (f) date(s) of ban; (g) date(s) of unban if any; (h) stated reason; (i) at least one primary-source URL. The Wikipedia tables already supply most of these for ~80% of entries.

5. **Tag schema additions.** Add binary flags for: religious-motivated, political-motivated, sexual/obscenity-motivated, LGBT-related, blasphemy, lèse-majesté, national-security, customs-import-ban, school-curriculum-removal, library-removal, author-prosecuted, author-imprisoned, author-killed, samizdat-circulated, burned. These unlock the most valuable cross-country queries (e.g. "all books banned for blasphemy across MENA," "all authors killed for their writing").

6. **Benchmarks for prioritising additions.** Add a country to the "deep-dive done" pile when ≥30 specific titles with sourced URLs are entered. Re-prioritise contemporary cases (post-2010) over historical because contemporary cases are under-documented elsewhere; the Index Librorum and Nazi blacklists are already well catalogued in academic literature, so cite-and-link rather than reproduce.

7. **Outsource gaps via diaspora communities.** For under-documented countries (Vietnam, Myanmar, Central Asian states, Lusophone Africa, Francophone Africa), partner with diaspora NGOs (e.g. The 88 Project for Vietnam — https://the88project.org; Justice for Myanmar; Reporters Without Borders local correspondents). They hold lists the English Wikipedia does not.

## Caveats

- **Numbers cited are approximate.** The "26,000 banned books in apartheid South Africa," "20,000 in Republican-era Turkey" (Karaca), "4,000+ on the Index," and "8,779 in 1944–48 Romania" (Petcu) come from researchers' counts rather than a single official register. Each ban often counts a re-edition separately; treat as orders-of-magnitude.
- **"Banned" is a spectrum.** This compilation uses the broadest definition (state ban, customs seizure, school removal, religious prohibition, court-ordered withdrawal, publisher self-withdrawal under threat). The database should preserve the distinction at the row level — conflating, e.g., a Hungarian school-curriculum sticker (Wonderland Is for Everyone) with a Nazi public book-burning would mislead users.
- **Wikipedia is not infallible.** Several entries in the master list bear "[citation needed]" or "[failed verification]" tags (e.g. Eritrea entries, *Mountain Wreath* in Bosnia). Flag such rows in the database with a `unverified` boolean and prioritise re-sourcing.
- **Some sources lean polemical.** The Iranian state's Ministry of Culture & Islamic Guidance does not publish its banned list, so reporting (RFE/RL, IranWire, CHRI) relies on author and publisher testimony — directionally accurate, but specific dates can shift. Saudi and PRC bans similarly under-documented; treat news reports as the best available evidence rather than gospel.
- **Future-tense or rumour items have been excluded.** The user should treat any entry that uses "may be banned," "could face," or "reportedly" with skepticism; the lists above use only completed bans.
- **English-language bias.** This compilation reflects English-language sources. Crucial primary lists in Spanish (Spain's *Index de libros prohibidos*), Russian (Glavlit registers, partially declassified), Arabic (Al-Azhar fatwa records), and Turkish (Karaca's catalogue) deserve dedicated translation work to deepen the archive.
- **Two intended tools (run_blocking_subagent and enrich_draft) referenced in research-process instructions were not actually present in the working environment;** consequently this report relies on direct web research only. The compilation is nonetheless comprehensive for a starting reference, but the user should expect to expand each country's section with several rounds of targeted follow-up using the source URLs above.
