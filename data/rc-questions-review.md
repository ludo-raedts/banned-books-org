# Reading Club questions — regeneration review

## 1984  
`rc_classics|book_id=4|book`

**Before:**
1. In 1984, the government constantly rewrites history to control the present. Do you see parallels today in the way information is shaped, filtered, or amplified through politics, media, or social platforms?
2. Orwell imagined a society where surveillance became normal and people adjusted their behavior because they might always be watched. How close do you think modern society is to that reality through smartphones, algorithms, cameras, and data tracking?
3. The concept of “doublethink” — holding two contradictory beliefs at once — is central to the novel. Can you think of modern examples where people or institutions publicly defend obvious contradictions?
4. Winston tries to preserve independent thought in a system designed to erase individuality. Do you think it is easier or harder today to maintain independent thinking in an age of constant information and online influence?
5. Why do you think 1984 continues to feel relevant decades after it was written? Which aspect of Orwell’s world feels most familiar — and which still feels exaggerated?

**After:**
1. The Party rewrites the past so that whoever controls the record controls reality itself. Where do you see the most unsettling modern version of that power — and is it held by governments, platforms, or all of us collectively?
2. Orwell makes surveillance feel ordinary: Winston simply assumes the telescreen is always watching and adjusts his face and movements accordingly. How much of your own behaviour is shaped by the assumption that you might be recorded, and have you stopped noticing it?
3. "Doublethink" — holding two contradictory beliefs at once and accepting both — is the Party's deepest tool. Where do you see people or institutions defending obvious contradictions today without any apparent discomfort?
4. The novel was banned both by the Soviet Union it seems to indict and, in 1976, by Argentina's military junta — and it is still challenged in US schools today. Why do regimes of such different stripes all find this same book dangerous?
5. Winston is certain his rebellion is doomed, yet he pursues it anyway. Is there real value in resistance you know will fail, or is that just a comfort the powerless tell themselves?
6. If a society could make its citizens genuinely contented by controlling what they are allowed to know, would anything important be lost — and if so, what exactly?

## The Trial  
`rc_classics|book_id=58|book`

**Before:**
1. How does Kafka's portrayal of the court system serve as a critique of bureaucratic power, and what emotions does this evoke in the reader?
2. In what ways does the character of Josef K. represent the existential struggles of modern man, and how do we see ourselves in his plight?
3. How does the absence of clear legal and moral guidelines in the novel enhance the feelings of paranoia and helplessness among characters and readers alike?
4. What is the significance of the novel’s setting being unnamed and abstract? How does this contribute to the story's universal themes?
5. Consider the roles of secondary characters in 'The Trial'. How do they contribute to Josef K.'s sense of alienation and confusion?
6. What might Kafka be saying about individual agency and identity in the face of oppressive systems?
7. How does 'The Trial' explore the tension between innocence and guilt, and how does this tension reflect on our own understandings of justice?
8. In considering 'The Trial's' history of censorship, what themes or ideas in the book might be seen as threatening to a society's status quo?
9. Reflect on how surveillance and the lack of privacy in 'The Trial' resonates with contemporary concerns about technology and surveillance in society.
10. Big Question: How does 'The Trial' challenge our understanding of justice, and what implications does this have for how we view authority and personal freedom today?

**After:**
1. Josef K. is never told what he is accused of, yet he keeps trying to mount a defence. What does the novel suggest about how people behave when the rules they are being judged by are deliberately withheld?
2. The authority in the book is everywhere and nowhere — offices in attics, officials who can decide nothing. Why might Kafka have made power banal and disorganised rather than grand and deliberate, and which version frightens you more?
3. Over the course of the book Josef K. drifts from indignation to resignation. Where did you feel that turn happen, and did it read as defeat, exhaustion, or a choice he made?
4. Kafka left the novel unfinished, and it was ordered and published by someone else after his death. Does reading a deliberately incomplete, posthumously assembled book change how much weight you are willing to give its meaning?
5. The Trial was banned as subversive by Nazi Germany in 1933 and again by Argentina's military junta in 1976 — both regimes that ran exactly the kind of opaque, accusatory machinery the novel describes. Why would an authoritarian state feel threatened by a story in which the system is absurd rather than evil?
6. When a person is told they are guilty but never told of what, is innocence still possible — or does living under permanent suspicion eventually make the accusation true?

5. Consider the portrayal of the female body and sexuality in the novel. How does Atwood challenge or reinforce societal norms through the experiences of the handmaids?

8. Why do you think 'The Handmaid's Tale' has been subject to censorship and challenges? What aspects of the book spark such strong reactions, and how does this alter its impact?

10. The final big question: What moral and ethical responsibilities do individuals have when confronting systemic oppression, and how can past dystopian narratives like 'The Handmaid's Tale' inform our understanding of present and future societal challenges?

**After:**

3. Gilead justifies controlling women's bodies as protection and even reverence. How do you tell the difference between a society that genuinely values something and one that uses that value as a cage?
4. The book is challenged in US schools on an unusually wide range of grounds at once — sexual, religious, political, and LGBTQ. What does it say about the novel that opponents from such different positions all want it gone?
5. Iran banned the book in 1985, the same year it appeared in the West. Why might a theocratic state and an American school board arrive at the same verdict on a novel about a theocracy?
6. Gilead is built and maintained by ordinary people who comply, adapt, or look away. How much of any oppressive system depends on the cooperation of people who do not think of themselves as cruel?

## The Satanic Verses

**Before:**
1. How does Rushdie use magical realism to blend reality with the surreal, and what does this technique suggest about the nature of truth and belief?
2. Consider the transformation of the characters Gibreel Farishta and Saladin Chamcha. How do their journeys explore the concept of identity and metamorphosis?
3. What role does the theme of duality play in the novel, particularly in the context of good versus evil, and how are these themes reflected in the characters' internal conflicts?
4. How does Rushdie portray religion and faith in the book, and what might this suggest about the relationship between personal belief systems and societal norms?
5. The novel sparked significant controversy and backlash. In what ways does Rushdie address the power structures within religious and political institutions, and why might this be unsettling or provocative to some readers?
6. Rushdie’s narrative style is distinctive and complex. How does the structure of the novel, with its interweaving stories and shifting perspectives, influence your understanding of its themes?
7. How do cultural and national identities shape the characters' experiences and conflicts within the novel, and what commentary might Rushdie be making about post-colonial societies?
8. In what ways does "The Satanic Verses" challenge the concept of freedom of expression, and how might this relate to contemporary debates over speech and censorship?
9. What are the implications of personal and cultural exile as portrayed in the novel, and how do these experiences influence the characters' sense of belonging?
10. In a world where diverse narratives often clash, what is the role of fiction in exploring and questioning deeply held beliefs about identity, faith, and society?

**After:**
1. The book was banned in dozens of countries within weeks of publication in 1988, often by governments whose officials had not read it. What does that tell you about how outrage over a book travels compared with the book itself?
2. Rushdie wrote a novel about migration, doubt, and identity, but it became known almost entirely for the offence some readers took. When a work's reputation eclipses its content this completely, can it ever be read on its own terms again?
3. The 1989 fatwa turned a literary controversy into a matter of life and death, and Rushdie lived under police protection for years. How should a society weigh an author's physical safety against the principle that books must be free to provoke?
4. Many who called for the ban argued they were defending the sacred from mockery. Is there a meaningful line between satire that punches at power and satire that wounds the deeply held beliefs of relatively powerless people?
5. Booksellers, translators, and publishers were attacked for their association with the book, not for writing it. What does a society owe to the people who merely print and distribute a dangerous work?
6. If a single book can put a writer's life at risk for decades, what does that cost reveal about the actual — as opposed to the stated — value a society places on free expression?

## The Tin Drum

**Before:**
1. Oskar refuses to grow physically after the age of three, even as the world around him descends into fascism and war. What do you think his refusal to “grow up” symbolizes within the context of German society before and during World War II?
2. The Tin Drum mixes absurdity, satire, fantasy, and historical trauma in unusual ways. Why do you think Günter Grass chose surrealism and dark humor instead of a more traditional war narrative?
3. Oskar is both an observer and participant in morally troubling events. Does the novel suggest that ordinary people are ever truly innocent during periods of authoritarianism and mass violence?

**After:**
1. Oskar chooses to stop growing at the age of three and watches the adult world from a child's height. How does that perspective let Grass say things about the rise of Nazism that an adult narrator might not?
2. Oskar is unreliable, self-serving, and at times complicit — not an innocent witness. Why might Grass have refused to give us a clean, sympathetic guide through this history?
3. The novel insists on the absurd, the grotesque, and the bodily even while describing grave historical events. What does that refusal of solemnity achieve that a more reverent treatment of the same period would miss?
4. Grass, who decades later revealed his own youthful service in the Waffen-SS, narrates a nation's reckoning from inside a mental institution. How does that framing shape how far we trust the account — and how a country remembers its past?
5. The book was restricted for sexual content in 1950s Germany and still challenged in the US as late as 1997. Does the offence taken at its sex and its body distract from the harder thing it asks Germans to look at?
6. When a whole society takes part in a catastrophe, is the honest way to remember it through solemn testimony — or through a narrator who is grotesque, complicit, and impossible to fully trust?
